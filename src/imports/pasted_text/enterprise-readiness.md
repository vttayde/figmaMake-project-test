Design a premium, narrative-led “Enterprise Readiness” page for InfoBeans.

This page sits under the About section, so it should not feel like a service page or a certification checklist. It should feel like a confidence-building story for CIOs, CTOs, IT Directors, procurement teams, and security stakeholders.

Core idea:
Enterprise buyers have many questions before they trust a technology partner. This page should pre-answer those questions through proof, structure, delivery maturity, and people.

Use:
- Lexend font family
- Lexend Light for body copy
- Lexend Regular for headings and section titles

Overall design direction:
- Premium, editorial, enterprise-grade
- Dark theme preferred
- Strong whitespace and confident typography
- Subtle red accent
- Thin dividers, structured cards, calm motion
- Avoid generic stock imagery
- Use abstract system visuals, proof cards, procurement-style checks, role cards, delivery timelines, and compact case proof blocks
- The page should feel like a trust story, not a brochure

Narrative flow:
Hidden concern → proof → enterprise checks → decision point → delivery maturity → practice depth → people → proof → final invitation

Page structure:

1. Hero: The question before the work begins

Purpose:
Open with the hidden concern enterprise buyers have before engaging any vendor.

Eyebrow:
ENTERPRISE READINESS

Headline:
Enterprise readiness, before you have to ask.

Supporting copy:
Before a project begins, enterprise teams are already looking for proof: security, scale, governance, delivery maturity, and the people who will actually do the work.

CTA:
Talk through readiness

Design:
- Large editorial headline
- Dark background
- Abstract system/grid visual
- Small proof tags around the hero
- Keep it calm, confident, and premium

2. Readiness Snapshot: First signals of trust

Purpose:
Give quick proof that InfoBeans can operate at enterprise scale.

Create 4 compact proof cards:
- 20+ years
  Building enterprise technology with long-term client partnerships.
- Global delivery
  Teams structured for distributed stakeholders, time zones, and delivery cycles.
- Cross-functional depth
  Design, engineering, AI, data, and platform teams working together.
- Enterprise platforms
  Experience across ServiceNow, Salesforce, cloud, data, AI, and custom digital products.

Design:
- Bento grid or proof strip
- Short copy
- Strong numbers and labels
- Should feel like quick confidence signals, not marketing claims

3. What Enterprise Teams Check First

Purpose:
Show that InfoBeans understands what slows enterprise buying down.

Headline:
The checks that happen before the first workshop.

Create 5 cards:
- Security & Compliance
  Policies, controls, certifications, and practices that help security teams move faster.
- Delivery Governance
  Clear ownership, checkpoints, documentation, escalation paths, and visible decision-making.
- Team Stability
  Experienced people, role clarity, and continuity from discovery through delivery.
- Platform Maturity
  Practical understanding of enterprise systems, integrations, governance, and long-term maintainability.
- Procurement Confidence
  The information buyers usually need, organized before conversations slow down.

Design:
- Large dark cards with subtle red edge or accent
- Each card should feel like an enterprise concern being answered
- Add hover state: “What this helps answer”
- Avoid generic corporate icons

4. Decision Point

Purpose:
After the reader has seen the main trust signals, give them a light decision moment. This should not feel like a sales banner. It should feel like a useful next step for enterprise teams already evaluating vendors.

Eyebrow:
READY TO COMPARE NOTES?

Headline:
If these are the checks your team cares about, we’re ready to talk through them.

Supporting copy:
Bring your security, procurement, platform, or delivery questions. We’ll help you understand what’s already in place and where more detail may be useful.

Primary CTA:
Talk through readiness

Secondary CTA:
Keep exploring

Design:
- Minimal, centered, spacious
- Not two heavy cards
- Should feel like a calm pause in the page
- Use subtle background shift or divider
- Keep it guided, not pushy

5. How Readiness Shows Up in Delivery

Purpose:
Move from claims to working behavior.

Headline:
Readiness is not a document. It shows up in delivery.

Use a 3-step narrative timeline:
- Before the work starts
  We align on goals, constraints, risks, stakeholders, platform state, and approval needs.
- During delivery
  Design, engineering, product, and client teams stay aligned through visible decisions and structured reviews.
- After launch
  Handoff, adoption, documentation, roadmap clarity, and long-term evolution stay part of the plan.

Design:
- Timeline or scroll-based story block
- Calm and spacious
- Should feel like how InfoBeans actually works

6. Depth Across Practices

Purpose:
Show InfoBeans has breadth without turning the page into a service catalogue.

Headline:
Different practices. One delivery rhythm.

Create 4 editorial blocks:
- Design & Experience
  Research, UX, visual systems, product thinking, and experience transformation.
- AI & Automation
  AI-assisted workflows, internal tools, copilots, automation, and operational intelligence.
- Enterprise Platforms
  ServiceNow, Salesforce, portals, workflows, integrations, and platform modernization.
- Engineering & Cloud
  Custom development, scalable architecture, cloud systems, APIs, and delivery execution.

Design:
- Clean grid
- Abstract line visuals or system patterns
- Each block should feel like depth, not sales

7. The People Behind the Work

Purpose:
Address the concern that the senior team disappears after the sale.

Headline:
The team you meet is the team that stays close.

Supporting copy:
Enterprise work needs continuity. Strategy, design, engineering, and delivery stay connected from the first conversation to handoff.

Design:
- Role-based cards instead of heavy biographies
- Example roles:
  Strategy Lead
  Design Lead
  Engineering Lead
  Delivery Lead
  Platform Architect
- Show role, experience, and involvement briefly
- Human but restrained

8. Proof in Practice

Purpose:
Show readiness through real work, not claims.

Headline:
Where readiness has already shown up.

Create 3 proof cards:
- ServiceNow transformation
  Improved employee self-service, reduced support dependency, and modernized the HRSD experience.
- AI workplace tools
  Designed internal AI workflows to improve speed, clarity, and decision support.
- Enterprise product modernization
  Reworked legacy platforms into clearer, more scalable digital experiences.

Design:
- Mini case cards
- Short and scannable
- Optional CTA: View related work

9. Closing CTA

Purpose:
End with confidence, not sales pressure.

Headline:
Ready before the first workshop.

Supporting copy:
If readiness is part of your decision, we can walk through the proof, process, and people behind the work.

Primary CTA:
Talk through readiness

Secondary CTA:
Explore our work

Design:
- Calm closing section
- Strong whitespace
- Avoid aggressive sales tone

Interaction ideas:
- Sticky anchor navigation:
  Trust, Checks, Delivery, Practices, People, Proof
- Slow ticker near the top:
  Security-ready • Governance-aware • Enterprise-scale • Platform depth • Design + engineering • Procurement confidence
- Hover states on readiness cards revealing “What this helps answer”
- Smooth scrolling and subtle reveal animations
- Avoid clutter and over-animation

Important:
The page should feel like a story of enterprise confidence. It should reduce buyer anxiety and make InfoBeans feel mature, safe, experienced, and easy to take forward internally.
